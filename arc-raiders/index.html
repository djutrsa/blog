Arc Raiders isn't dying of bad design. It's dying of opportunity cost.

By David Jutrsa · June 11, 2026 · 10 min read

12,000,000
Copies sold · first ten weeks
69,021
Peak players on Steam · June 2026
All-Time Low

How does a game sell 12 million copies in 10 weeks, win a Game Award, and reach 960,000 concurrent players across all platforms, only to bottom out 5 months later at 69,021 players on Steam, an all-time low?

Nearly all commentary about Arc Raiders gives the same answer: Cheaters. I don't buy it. The cheaters are a symptom. The real answer sits across a Tokyo earnings call, a Stockholm hiring plan, and a promotion announcement that almost nobody has read. This is my take on the story of what actually happened, as well as I can piece it together from public numbers.

The curve is weird

It helps to start by disposing of the lazy take, the one where Arc Raiders is "simply dying the way every live-service game dies". The boring truth is that an 80% drop from launch peak is normal. Most live-service games lose 70-90% of their players within two or three months. Helldivers 2 did it, Battlefield 6 did it, and Borderlands 4 lost 95%. If that were the whole story, this post wouldn't exist.

What makes Arc Raiders worth writing about (other than its fun gameplay) is the shape of its curve. The game peaked in November at ~482,000 concurrent players on Steam (~960,000 counting all platforms). December brought the usual holiday dip. Then, in January, Arc did something games almost never do: it climbed back up to 466,000. Ten weeks after launch, Arc Raiders was essentially at full strength. Decaying games don't do that. This was a game with a real audience.

But then the floor gave out. February fell 20%, March fell 33%, and April fell 39%, the steepest drop to that point and a full five months after launch. May dropped another 21%, and June opened at a new low and kept sliding.

Hype decay is front-loaded. The "tourists" leave early and the curve flattens out, but Arc's curve did the opposite — it accelerated after the hype window closed. The people leaving in March weren't tourists. They were the audience that came back in January. A decline that accelerates that late suggests something specific pushed those players out, and the streamers were the first place where it showed.

The canary

Arc Raiders blew up partly because streamers carried it, and that created a loop nobody designed: the bigger your stream, the worse your game gets. Popularity in an extraction shooter makes you a target, because stream snipers can use the broadcast itself to follow you into matches — with Arc, the stream snipers also had cheats in hand. As an Arc streamer, your reward for loving and supporting this game (and doing so loudly) was suddenly a worse playing experience.

By January, the problem had boiled over. A viral clip showed a cheater openly explaining that getting caught only earned him a thirty-day timeout — anyone who has played online knows this is not a deterrent, more so a pause. @Ninja spent the winter publicly urging Embark to act, and by April he was gone, citing cheaters and stream snipers (his brief comeback attempt later that month for the Riven Tides update lasted ~3 hours before he walked away again). @Nadeshot, a man who survived peak Call of Duty lobbies, said cheating in Arc might be worse, and he wanted Embark to run Epic's Fortnite playbook by taking the cheat sellers to court.

Embark did respond, with promised anti-cheat overhauls, new detection, and a three-stage ban system that the community immediately nicknamed "a slap on the wrist". Meanwhile, a quieter complaint kept growing underneath the cheating noise: the new content everyone was begging for wasn't coming.

Every multiplayer game fights cheaters, as that is the cost of doing business. What makes this case interesting is the math around it. Arc Raiders made its publisher ~half a billion dollars in three months by analyst estimates. The problem was both visible and solvable, plus Epic Games had literally published the playbook. So why couldn't a studio printing money kill a solvable problem? To answer that, you need to know who owns Embark, and this is where the story stops being about a video game.

The small team is the product

When people ask why Embark didn't simply scale up, hire aggressively, raise money, and go "full Fortnite", they're picturing Embark as Epic in 2017, independent and master of its own destiny. Embark is neither. It has been a wholly-owned subsidiary of Nexon since before its first game shipped. Nexon put in $41 million at the studio's founding in 2018, took majority control in 2019, and completed the buyout in 2021, for a total of roughly $300 million. Nexon's own investors hated the deal at the time, and the CEO who pushed it through later admitted that analysts called him crazy for buying an unprofitable Western studio with zero shipped games.

So there is no fundraising lever here. While Epic could sell a stake to Tencent, the best Embark could do is submit an internal budget request to Nexon.

The small team isn't a limitation Embark is stuck with. The small team is the product Nexon bought.

But I believe the deeper issue is more nuanced: The small team isn't a limitation Embark is stuck with. The small team is the product Nexon bought. Embark was founded by ex-DICE leadership on a single bet: Modern tooling will let a few hundred people build what used to take a few thousand. Embark Studios employs ~300 people, has shipped two AAA titles, and took Arc Raiders toe-to-toe with Call of Duty on a fraction of the headcount. Nexon holds that up internally as the proof of concept its other studios should learn from. If Embark tripled its headcount to "save" Arc Raiders, it would falsify the very thesis that makes Embark valuable to its owner. The organization cannot hire its way out without contradicting its own strategy memo.

The money already happened

The obvious objection is that money should force the issue, because a dying golden goose ought to set off alarms at Nexon. The trouble is that the money had already happened.

Fortnite was free to play (F2P), which meant Epic's revenue was the player count. Every lost player was lost income, so churn was existential, and Epic responded existentially. Arc Raiders, by contrast, was a $40 box. By the end of Q1 of 2026 it had sold a total of 15.5 million copies (4.6 million in that quarter alone) and crossed 16 million copies by the May earnings report — all while the Steam player count was collapsing. Players were leaving, but copies were still flying off the shelves, so Nexon was posting the best quarter in its 30-year history, with Western revenue up more than 300%. Even engagement looked fine by Nexon's own numbers: 6 million weekly active players, more than half of them past a hundred hours.

In other words, Nexon's dashboard was green. Revenue was a record and engagement was strong; while Steam concurrent players were falling, Steam concurrents rarely appear on an income statement. For a paid game, churn is a reputation problem rather than a revenue problem.

Nexon didn't shrug, though. It did something stranger. In May, the day before reporting that record quarter, Embark announced it was cutting Arc Raiders' update schedule in half. To understand that decision, you need the Warzone story.

The Warzone counterfactual

In March 2020, Call of Duty: Warzone launched and blew up, reaching 50 million players in its first month and 100 million in just over a year. Assigning the Battle Royale mode to the "support team" (Raven) while the "A-Team" (Infinity Ward) focused on the $60 premium Modern Warfare title signaled that Activision initially viewed Warzone as an add-on value for the main game, not necessarily the future of the franchise. Yet, after Battle Royale games exploded, Activision's response became a case study in seizing the moment: The company swarmed Warzone. Raven Software kept the lead, while High Moon and Beenox rotated on, and half the Call of Duty ecosystem ended up pointed at a game that was initially a side project. Activision could do that because it had something priceless: A dozen studios already trained on the same engine, the same pipeline, and the same production culture. The "reinforcements" already knew the codebase.

So why didn't Nexon swarm Arc Raiders? Cash certainly wasn't the problem — Nexon ended 2025 sitting on ~$5 billion. The problem is that money can't buy what Activision had. Nexon has no other Western AAA studios, and nobody else on Earth ships games on Embark's proprietary tech stack. Brooks's Law is undefeated: Throwing 200 new hires at a live game = output slows for 12-18 months while they learn the codebase. Money buys headcount, but it can't buy a second team that already knows where the bodies are buried.

What makes this genuinely strange is that keeping games alive for decades is Nexon's superpower. MapleStory is 22 years old and still growing, and Dungeon & Fighter is 20 years old (and freshly renewed in China for another 10). This company doesn't do hype cycles; it does "forever-games". One of the world's best "golden goose farmers" had a brand-new golden goose in Arc Raiders and chose not to farm it, and the reason traces back to a design decision that was completely correct... until it wasn't.

The loop debt

The genre king of extraction shooters is Escape from Tarkov, and Tarkov's grip on people comes down to one word: variance. To over-simplify, Tarkov stacks its variance loop in four layers.

The first layer is "time to kill" (TTK). In Tarkov, death comes fast and from anywhere. One mistake or one bad angle and you're gone, along with all of the loot you carried. That is where the famous "Tarkov anxiety" lives, something no other shooter has quite replicated.

The second layer is the loot. Item values aren't a gentle curve but a lottery. A lot of what you find is junk or not useful in the moment; and then, you open a med case, see a LEDX, and your heart stops — that one item is worth more than your last 20 raids combined. It's not just about the money, it's also because some of the rarest items (like LEDX) gate endgame progression. You need them, so finding one — and extracting with it — leads to genuine physical glee.

The third layer, the economy, converts that lottery into power: you sell the rare find (or upgrade your stash) and buy/upgrade your kit. The fourth layer is that the kit actually matters. Better armor and better ammo shift your time to kill, and you take that gear into a raid where the first layer is waiting. One bullet sends you back to zero, shaking and already planning the next raid. Anxiety feeds glee, glee feeds power, and power feeds anxiety again. That loop holds people for thousands of hours.

In Arc Raiders, every one of those dials is flattened. Time to kill is compressed to the point that a "free kit" (zero risk) can drop a fully-geared player by design. Loot value also sits in a narrow band, so nothing you find makes your pulse spike. Progressing to the endgame ("expedition") is a calm, linear grind, and the endgame, when you finally get there, is currently non-existent.

The twist that most Arc Raiders takes miss is that this flattening wasn't a mistake. It was the acquisition strategy. Tarkov's big random swings feel completely different depending on who you are. A Tarkov beginner has no map knowledge, no inventory stockpile, and no idea which items matter, so the variance mostly deletes their stuff over time. They experience variance as pure punishment. A Tarkov veteran with 2,000 hours has the skill and the reserves to absorb the losses, so the same randomness becomes the source of their best moments: the jackpot LEDX find, the narrow escape, the high-stakes kill.

But this loop is also Tarkov's filter; because it tortures beginners, Tarkov has never sold 16 million copies. Embark sanded the variance off on purpose, and that is exactly why 4 million people bought Arc Raiders in two weeks and allowed streamers to carry it. It was the right call, and it was also a loan. A flat loop is perfect for your first 60 hours. Past hour 100 (where half of Arc Raiders' player base lives), flat means no new stories worth telling and no endgame worth grinding for.

In my view, the heartbreaking part is that Arc Raiders' possibilities are endless. The mechanics are both deep and fun; and there is a whole quest system with lore that reads as a mix of Horizon Zero Dawn, Terminator, and something unique, yet the quests barely move the story forward.

The systems team built something vast, but the content team never got the headcount to furnish it.

Nobody builds that scaffolding planning to abandon it. The systems team built something vast, but the content team never got the headcount to furnish it. That brings us back to the announcement in May.

Two updates a year, two new games

On May 13, 2026, Embark announced that Arc Raiders was moving from monthly updates to 2 per year, with the next major content drop in October. That meant 5 months without a major update for a game already bleeding players. The very next day, Nexon reported the best quarter in company history, driven by Arc Raiders.

Buried in the same news cycle was the sentence that helps explain this: Embark has two new, unannounced projects in development. The constraint was never Nexon's 5 billion in cash. It is the ~300 people who actually know Embark's tech stack, the scarcest developers in the building, and they are being pointed at the "next Arc Raiders" instead of this one. Activision's answer to Warzone was to deepen the hit; Nexon's answer to Arc Raiders is to replicate the studio that made it.

Let's briefly run the expected value on that choice. Deepening a proven hit, with 16 million game owners, 6 million weekly actives, and demand sitting right there, is as close to a sure thing as one can get. A new live-service IP is a lottery ticket; even great studios land maybe one or two in ten, and the graveyard is enormous. Embark's own track record also confirms this. "The Finals", the studio's first game, is alive — flat on players but with revenue up ~50% YoY in Arc's halo; however, at Arc's peak, The Finals was more than 20x smaller, and the gap has since narrowed — mostly because Arc kept shrinking. One hit (Arc) and one survivor (Finals) isn't "a Midas touch". It's a coin that came up heads once.

Arc Raiders isn't dying of bad design. It's dying of opportunity cost. That leaves one final question: who made that call?

The hypothesis

I want to be both honest and careful here, because what follows is a guess and nobody at Nexon has said it on the record. But it's interesting to contrast it against the timeline.

In February 2026, Patrick Söderlund, Embark's founder and CEO, was promoted to Executive Chairman of Nexon itself and tasked with leading global expansion. He still runs Embark on paper, but his job is now all of Nexon. Consider what that means. The person allocating Embark's developers, the one deciding whether they deepen Arc Raiders or build the next thing, is a founder whose whole identity (going back to his DICE days), is making new games. He walked away from EA, one of the biggest publishers on Earth, months after it offered him a $20 million retention package, all to build something of his own. To me, this signals that running live operations on an already-successful extraction shooter is not why this man gets out of bed.

So, the fox isn't in the henhouse. The fox is doing the henhouse's capital allocation. The belief that "you don't need a golden goose (Arc) when you have the Midas touch" is exactly a thing a founder like Söderlund may hold in his bones, and is also exactly the thing a CFO cannot disprove until the second Embark swing misses.

The honest counterweight is that Embark says it is hiring, and its stated reason for the slowdown is update quality. Nobody on the record says resources moved away from Arc, which is why this only stays a hypothesis with the timeline as the only witness.

All the raw material for a decade-long game is sitting right there in the build, the quests, the lore, the systems... and the only people who could use it are down the hall, now apparently making something else. The decision may be defensible from Stockholm, but it's heartbreaking from the couch. And that part never makes the patch notes.
