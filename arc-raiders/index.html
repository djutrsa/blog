Arc Raiders isn't dying of bad design. It's dying of opportunity cost.

June 11, 2026 · 9 min read

All-Time Low

How does a game sell twelve million copies in ten weeks, win a Game Award, and reach 960,000 concurrent players, only to bottom out five months later at 69,021 players on Steam, an all-time low?

Nearly every video essay about Arc Raiders gives the same answer: cheaters. I don't buy it. The cheaters are a symptom. The real answer sits in a Tokyo earnings call, a Stockholm hiring plan, and a promotion announcement that almost nobody read. This is the story of what actually happened, as well as I can piece it together from public numbers.

The curve is weird

It helps to start by disposing of the lazy take, the one where Arc Raiders is simply dying the way every live-service game dies. The boring truth is that an 80 percent drop from launch peak is normal. Most live-service games lose 70 to 90 percent of their players within two or three months. Helldivers 2 did it, Battlefield 6 did it, and Borderlands 4 lost 95 percent. If that were the whole story, this post wouldn't exist.

What makes Arc Raiders worth writing about is the shape of its curve. The game peaked in November at around 482,000 concurrent players on Steam, roughly 960,000 counting all platforms. December brought the usual holiday dip. Then January did something games almost never do: it climbed back up, to 466,000. Ten weeks after launch, Arc Raiders was essentially at full strength. Decaying games don't do that. This was a game with a real audience.

Then the floor gave out. February fell 20 percent, March fell 33, and April fell 39, the worst month yet and a full five months after launch. May dropped another 21, and June opened at a new low and kept sliding.

Hype decay is front-loaded. The tourists leave early and the curve flattens out, but Arc's curve did the opposite and accelerated after the hype window closed. The people leaving in March weren't tourists. They were the audience that came back in January. A decline that accelerates that late isn't gravity. Something specific pushed those players out, and the streamers were the first place it showed.

The canary

Arc Raiders blew up partly because streamers carried it, and that created a loop nobody designed: the bigger your stream, the worse your game gets. Popularity in an extraction shooter makes you a target, because stream snipers can use the broadcast itself to follow you into matches, cheats in hand. Your reward for loving this game loudly was a worse version of it.

By January the problem had boiled over. A viral clip showed a cheater openly explaining that getting caught only earned him a thirty-day timeout, which is not a deterrent so much as a subscription pause. Ninja spent the winter publicly begging Embark to act, and by April he was gone, citing cheaters and snipers. Nadeshot, a man who survived peak Call of Duty lobbies, said this might be worse, and he wanted Embark to run Epic's Fortnite playbook by taking the cheat sellers to court.

Embark did respond, with promised anti-cheat overhauls, new detection, and a three-stage ban system that the community immediately nicknamed a slap on the wrist. Meanwhile a quieter complaint kept growing underneath the cheating noise: the content everyone was begging for wasn't coming.

Every multiplayer game fights cheaters, and that is the cost of doing business. What makes this case interesting is the math around it. Arc Raiders made its publisher roughly half a billion dollars in three months, by analyst estimates. The problem was visible and solvable, and Epic had literally published the playbook. So why couldn't a studio printing money kill a solvable problem? To answer that, you need to know who owns Embark, and this is where the story stops being about a video game.

The small team is the product

When people ask why Embark didn't simply scale up, hire aggressively, raise money, and go full Fortnite, they're picturing Embark as Epic in 2017, independent and master of its own destiny. Embark is neither. It has been a wholly owned subsidiary of Nexon since before its first game shipped. Nexon put in $41 million at the studio's founding in 2018, took majority control in 2019, and completed the buyout in 2021, for a total of roughly $300 million. Nexon's own investors hated the deal at the time, and the CEO who pushed it through later admitted that analysts called him crazy for buying an unprofitable Western studio with zero shipped games.

So there is no fundraising lever here. Epic could sell a stake to Tencent. Embark can submit a budget request to Tokyo.

The small team isn't a limitation Embark is stuck with. The small team is the product Nexon bought.

The deeper issue took me a while to see. The small team isn't a limitation Embark is stuck with. The small team is the product Nexon bought. Embark was founded by ex-DICE leadership on a single bet, that modern tooling lets a few hundred people build what used to take a few thousand. The studio employs roughly 300 people, has shipped two AAA titles, and took Arc Raiders toe to toe with Call of Duty on a fraction of the headcount. Nexon holds that up internally as the proof of concept its other studios should learn from.

Now follow the logic. If Embark triples its headcount to save Arc Raiders, it falsifies the very thesis that makes Embark valuable to its owner. The organization cannot hire its way out without contradicting its own strategy memo.

The money already happened

The obvious objection is that money should force the issue, because a dying golden goose ought to set off alarms in Tokyo. The trouble is that the money had already happened.

Fortnite was free to play, which meant Epic's revenue was the player count. Every lost player was lost income, churn was existential, and Epic responded existentially. Arc Raiders, by contrast, was a forty-dollar box. By the end of the first quarter of 2026 it had sold 15.5 million copies, 4.6 million in that quarter alone, and it crossed sixteen million by the May earnings report, all while the Steam player count was collapsing. Players were leaving, copies were still flying off the shelves, and Nexon was posting the best quarter in its thirty-year history, with Western revenue up more than 300 percent. Even engagement looked fine by Nexon's own numbers: six million weekly active players, more than half of them past a hundred hours.

From Tokyo, in other words, the dashboard was green. Revenue was a record and engagement was strong, and while Steam concurrents were falling, Steam concurrents do not appear on an income statement. For a paid game, churn is a reputation problem rather than a revenue problem.

Nexon didn't shrug, though. It did something stranger. In May, the day before reporting that record quarter, Embark announced it was cutting Arc Raiders' update schedule in half. To understand that decision, you need the Warzone story.

The Warzone counterfactual

In March 2020, Call of Duty: Warzone launched as a side project and detonated, reaching fifty million players in its first month and a hundred million in just over a year. Activision's response became the canonical case study in seizing a moment: the company swarmed it. Raven Software took the lead, High Moon and Beenox rotated on, and half the Call of Duty ecosystem ended up pointed at one game. Activision could do that because it had something priceless, a dozen studios already trained on the same engine, the same pipeline, and the same production culture. The reinforcements already knew the codebase.

So why didn't Nexon swarm Arc Raiders? Cash wasn't the problem. Nexon ended 2025 sitting on roughly five billion dollars. The problem is that money can't buy what Activision had. Nexon has no other Western AAA studios, and nobody else on Earth ships games on Embark's proprietary tech stack. Brooks's Law is undefeated: throw two hundred new hires at a live game and output slows for eighteen months while they learn the codebase. Capital buys headcount, not a second team that already knows where the bodies are buried.

What makes this genuinely strange is that keeping games alive for decades is Nexon's superpower. MapleStory is twenty-two years old and still growing, and Dungeon & Fighter is twenty, freshly renewed in China for another ten. This company doesn't do hype cycles; it does forever-games. The world's best goose farmers had a brand-new golden goose and chose not to farm it, and the reason traces back to a design decision that was completely correct until it wasn't.

The loop debt

The genre king here is Escape from Tarkov, and Tarkov's grip on people comes down to one word: variance. The game stacks it in four layers.

The first layer is time to kill. In Tarkov, death comes fast and from anywhere. One mistake or one bad angle and you're gone, along with everything you carried. That is where the famous Tarkov anxiety lives. Your hands shake on a loot run, and no other shooter does that to people.

The second layer is the loot. Item values aren't a gentle curve but a lottery. Most of what you find is junk, and then you open a med case, see a LedX, and your heart stops, because that one item is worth more than your last twenty raids combined. It isn't even just the money, since the rarest items gate endgame progression. You need them, and finding one is physical glee.

The third layer, the economy, converts that lottery into power: you sell the rare find and buy the endgame kit. The fourth layer is that the kit actually matters. Better armor and better ammo shift your time to kill, and you take that gear into a raid where the first layer is waiting. One bullet sends you back to zero, shaking and already planning the next LedX hunt. Anxiety feeds glee, glee feeds power, and power feeds anxiety again. That loop holds people for thousands of hours.

In Arc Raiders, every one of those dials is flattened. Time to kill is compressed, to the point that a free starter kit can drop a fully geared player by design. Loot value sits in a narrow band, so nothing you find makes your pulse spike. Progression is a calm, linear grind, and the endgame, when you finally get there, is currently non-existent.

The honest turn, and the one most takes miss, is that the flattening wasn't a mistake. It was the acquisition strategy. Variance is regressive, in that it transfers fun from new players to invested ones. Tarkov's loop is also Tarkov's filter; it tortures beginners, and it has never sold sixteen million copies of anything. Embark sanded the variance off on purpose, and that is exactly why four million people bought this game in two weeks and streamers could carry it.

It was the right call, and it was also a loan. A flat loop is perfect for your first sixty hours. Past hour one hundred, where half this player base lives, flat means no stories worth telling and no chase worth grinding. The bill comes due at the endgame, and the endgame is bare.

The systems team built a cathedral, and the content team never got the headcount to furnish it.

The heartbreaking part is that the vault is full. There is a whole quest system in this game, and lore that reads as one part Horizon Zero Dawn, one part Terminator, and one part something all its own, yet the quests barely move it forward. There is mechanical depth everywhere. Nobody builds that scaffolding planning to abandon it. The systems team built a cathedral, and the content team never got the headcount to furnish it. That brings us back to the announcement in May.

Two updates a year, two new games

On May 13, 2026, Embark announced that Arc Raiders was moving from monthly updates to two per year, with the next major content drop in October. That meant five months without a major update for a game already bleeding players. The very next day, Nexon reported the best quarter in company history, driven by Arc Raiders. The announcements came twenty-four hours apart, and the slowdown came first.

Buried in the same news cycle was the sentence that explains everything: Embark has two new, unannounced projects in development. The constraint was never Nexon's five billion in cash. It is the three hundred or so people who actually know Embark's tech stack, the scarcest developers in the building, and they are being pointed at the next Arc Raiders instead of this one. Activision's answer to Warzone was to deepen the hit; Nexon's answer to Arc Raiders is to replicate the studio that made it.

Run the expected value on that choice. Deepening a proven hit, with sixteen million owners, six million weekly actives, and demand sitting right there, is close to a sure thing. A new live-service IP is a lottery ticket; even great studios land maybe one or two in ten, and the graveyard is enormous. Embark's own track record says the quiet part. The Finals, the studio's first game, is alive and even growing a little in Arc's halo, but at Arc's peak it was more than twenty times smaller, and the gap has narrowed since mostly because Arc kept shrinking. One hit and one survivor isn't a Midas touch. It's a coin that came up heads once.

Arc Raiders isn't dying of bad design. It's dying of opportunity cost. That leaves one final question: who made that call?

The hypothesis

I want to be careful here, because what follows is a guess and nobody at Nexon has said it on the record. But watch the timeline.

In February 2026, Patrick Söderlund, Embark's founder and CEO, was promoted to Executive Chairman of Nexon itself and tasked with leading global expansion. He still runs Embark on paper, but his job is now all of Nexon. Consider what that means. The person allocating Embark's developers, the one deciding whether they deepen Arc Raiders or build the next thing, is a founder whose whole identity, going back to his DICE days, is making new games. He walked away from one of the biggest publishers on Earth, months after it handed him a twenty-million-dollar retention package, to build something of his own. Running live operations on a five-year-old extraction shooter is not why this man gets out of bed.

The fox isn't in the henhouse. The fox is doing the henhouse's capital allocation. The belief that you don't need a golden goose when you have the Midas touch is exactly the thing a founder holds in his bones, and exactly the thing a CFO cannot disprove until the second swing misses.

The honest counterweight is that Embark says it is hiring, and its stated reason for the slowdown is update quality. Nobody on the record says resources moved away from this game. That is exactly why this stays a hypothesis, and why the timeline is the only witness.

Here is where it lands, then. All the raw material for a decade-long game is sitting right there in the build, the quests and the lore and the systems, and the only people who could use it are down the hall, making something else. The decision is defensible from Stockholm and heartbreaking from the couch. That part never makes the patch notes.
